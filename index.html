A plain-language dashboard for understanding rainfall deficit, vegetation condition, and early agricultural stress signals in Papua New Guinea. It is designed for briefings, field-verification planning, and government advisory work, because apparently data is only useful when people can actually understand it.

What this dashboard is for

In simple terms: this dashboard helps answer three questions: Where has rainfall been low? Where is vegetation showing stress? and which provinces should be checked first on the ground?

This is an early warning and screening tool, not a final drought declaration. The data points to possible areas of concern. Government officers, DAL provincial teams, disaster offices, and partners should then verify conditions in the field: crop condition, water availability, food gardens, livestock, fires, market prices, and community reports.

Plain-language guide to the indicators

Rainfall anomaly
Shows whether rainfall is above or below the long-term average. Negative values mean drier-than-normal conditions.
NDVI anomaly
Shows whether vegetation is greener or less green than usual. Negative values can mean vegetation is stressed or drying.
VHI
Vegetation Health Index combines vegetation condition and temperature stress. Lower values suggest poorer vegetation health.
ASI
Agricultural Stress Index estimates the percentage of crop area under stress. Higher values mean more cropland is likely affected.
Important: one bad indicator does not automatically mean drought. A rainfall deficit may appear before crops show stress. Vegetation may stay green for a while after rain drops. ASI is stronger evidence for agricultural concern, but it still needs field confirmation.

How to explain this to non-technical users

Suggested wording:

The satellite data shows early signs of dry conditions and possible agricultural stress in some provinces. These results help us decide where to verify crop and water conditions first. They do not replace field reports from farmers and provincial officers.

Avoid saying: “This province is officially in drought.”
Say instead: “This province is a priority for field verification.”

What field teams should verify

  • Are food gardens drying, wilting, or failing?
  • Are water sources low, dry, salty, or unsafe?
  • Are households changing meals or relying on wild foods?
  • Are market prices rising for staple foods?
  • Are fires, pests, or livestock problems increasing?
  • Can photos and GPS points confirm the situation?

This is where satellite data becomes useful instead of just being pretty pixels in a meeting slide.

Government-facing insight

Recommended next actions

  1. Verify priority provinces: contact DAL provincial officers, NDPM, and provincial administrations for field reports.
  2. Use Earth Map: cross-check rainfall, vegetation, land cover, fire, and surface water for priority areas.
  3. Use SEPAL: build a repeatable workflow so the same indicators can be updated every dekad or month.
  4. Prepare a short monthly note: include current data, maps, field reports, and practical advisory actions.
Limitation: this dashboard uses uploaded province-level data. It is useful for national and provincial screening, but district and LLG-level decisions need finer boundaries, local crop calendars, and field reports.
